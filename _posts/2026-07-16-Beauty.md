>"The reason that I cannot really say that I positively enjoy *nature* is that I do not quite realise *what* it is that I enjoy. A work of art, on the other hand, I can grasp, I can — if I may put it this way — find that Archimedean point, and as soon as I have found it, everything is readily clear for me. Then I am able to pursue this one main idea and see how all the details serve to illuminate it. I see the author’s whole individuality as if it were the sea, in which every single detail is reflected. The author’s spirit is kindred to me... The works of the deity are too great for me; I always get lost in the details." ~ *(JP §117)*

"Life is beautiful."

Unfortunately, that sentence is repeated so often, with so little meaning, that it is merely a platitude.  It is such a profound statement, but on a day to day basis, it is hollow. Perhaps this is because 'beauty' means very little. To many people, beauty exists merely in the transient pull and push of our emotions: the rush of love, the suffocation of sadness, the swell of laughter... We are gripped by powerful, inexplicable emotion, and then when it leaves us, we are left empty and poor, just as before. If beauty is this sensation, this transient, fleeting thing, then it is a cursed fruit, because it is unsatiating. The problem with this understanding of beauty is the lie of its transience. 

### On satiety in meaning:  
I have no quarrel with the notion that beauty is the fruit of our emotions, but I am inclined to believe that satiating beauty is a fruit that only emerges with the labour of our perception and thought. Its full depth of flavour is in understanding *why* a beautiful object evokes such awe and wonder?. The prick of sensation when we perceive beauty is transient, but the satisfaction of understanding the meaning and purpose that makes something beautiful is persistent and nourishing. Perhaps to cherish a beautiful thing is to ponder over its meaning - it is only in this case that I am left without regret, when the object of my admiration withers away.

### On depth in meaning:
To ponder over the meaning of something beautiful is to also enrich its beauty. If beauty is somehow the corollary of harmony and order (which are not antithetic to complexity and micro-chaos), then surely meaning and purpose, which bring harmony and order to the heart, are the most beautiful things of all. And so reflecting on something's beauty not only frees it of its impermanence, but also enriches it with a far deeper and more profound beauty.

This is what I think people long for when they say "you never realise how beautiful something is until it's gone". They yearn for the satiety and depth that understanding the meaning of a beautiful thing brings. If they had appreciated what makes the object of their admiration beautiful, perhaps they would not regret their neglect.

### On divinity and beauty:  
But what is meaningful? What gives things meaning? I believe that all meaning comes from and ends in God. God gives all things their meaning and purpose. And if all meaning leads to God, then to perceive the highest beauty in everything is to see an actualisation of God's will - to truly admire beauty is to say “praise be to God (الحمد لله)”. In this deeper admiration, this deeper understanding of the divine awe that our emotions evoke, there is lasting satisfaction unlike any other.

### On perception and beauty:
This understanding of beauty is a bipartite one, because beauty can exist in two forms:
1. **Beauty exists independently of our perception.** Something can be objectively beautiful even if there are no mortal souls to admire it, because objectivity comes from God, and God's perception is eternal and all-encompassing.
2. **Beauty exists in our perception.** There are two deeper meanings to this. Firstly, the vibrancy of our sensory faculties - our literal ability to perceive beauty - is in itself a beautiful thing. Regardless of the object of perception, one can be awestruck by this blessing. Secondly, any circumstance can be perceived as beautiful within a religious framework:

If beauty is a matter of perception, then why should we choose to see beauty? Well, if humans are created for the sole purpose of worshiping God, then to see the world as beautiful, and to be reminded of God, and his praise, is a fulfillment of that purpose. This is perhaps why "there is good for him in it", as the aforementioned hadith says.

### Is impermanence meaningful?
The framing of beauty that I have provided is 'meaning in spite of impermanence', but I have heard many atheists repeat the aphorism: 'impermanence gives things meaning' and therefore 'impermanence makes things beautiful'. But I see no reason for impermanence to be meaningful, in an atheist framework, where nothing has objective worth. 

But what about in a theistic framing? Let's non-axiomatically consider the notion that 'impermanence gives things meaning'. Firstly, I think that this statement is an assertion of 'rarity' as beauty. So we should really be asking 'if something is rare/scarce, either spatially or temporally, does that make it more precious?' My intuition seems to agree with this statement, and I can think of two examples where this seems to be true:
1. People buy limited-time, collector's items *because* they are numerically rare/temporally rare (the latter being impermanence), so they seem to attribute worth to rarity.
2. People love their significant other more, because they are perceived as special, unique and irreplaceable, so they seemingly value the rarity/uniqueness of their loved one.

But upon deeper examination, I don't believe that my intuition holds in either case.
1. The first case seems to be an irrational conflation of situational worth and objective worth. When considering a system constrained by supply and demand, as supply decreases, for any given demand, the situational worth of an object will increase. But here its situational value is strictly tied to 'demand'. Its value is not tied intrinsically to its rarity. If demand is not a constraining factor, then the objective worth of a bag of rice does not change depending on whether there are 100,000 bags of rice, or merely 100, because in both cases its utility (which is tied to its function and therefore purpose) is the same.
2. The second case is a conflation of specificity and rarity. When one speaks of the 'one of a kind specialness' of love, they are in awe of the 'specificity' of their lover, not their 'scarcity'. The fact that they believe that this person is "perfect for them" is a statement on the beauty of God's chosen, specific, teleological complementarity between two individuals, not a statement on their rarity or scarcity. To better elucidate this point, if their lover had an identical twin, with exactly the same qualities, would that change their beauty and objective value? No. In this case, we have devalued rarity, but the specific qualities which make that person beautiful persist.  
What about temporal rarity? Does the fragility and transience of life make someone or something more beautiful? No. By putting a temporal constraint on the time that we have with someone or something that is beautiful, we are motivated to appreciate their beauty more intensely/carefully, before our time with them expires, but just like demand, time is a constraint on our ability to appreciate beauty, not a source of beauty itself. 

But I have to give some credit to impermanence, because this world is not eternal. Autumn leaves are not more beautiful because they must wither away, *but* the fact that they will wither should motivate their admirer to appreciate them, before they are gone. Similarly, if we are certain that there is no meaning without God, we ought to serve God with all our heart before our time comes. The fleetingness of life should evoke fear and motivate us to use our precious time wisely, before it runs out...

### On beauty:
For these reasons, I am led to believe that beauty is not in transience, it is in spite of transience. And whilst the first prick of our emotions is the precipice of beauty, true beauty is found deeper, in meaning: true beauty is found in God. And so to truly admire beauty is to say “praise be to God (الحمد لله)”.


### Problems with perceiving beauty:
I believe that I have given sufficient substantiation to show that, to a believer, "life is beautiful" (without falling into platitudes). But maybe I can make this sentence even more meaningful by pointing out the various traps and potholes that one might fall into when perceiving beauty:

For a long time I felt as Kierkegaard does when he says:

>"The reason that I cannot really say that I positively enjoy *nature* is that I do not quite realise *what* it is that I enjoy"

He writes of this sickness of perception, where one is able to recognise that what they are seeing *is* noteworthy, juxtaposed by a feeling of dissatisfaction at not being able to identify the cause of its beauty. If one lacks the depth of perception to identify meaning (and thus divine will) in the object of their admiration, then they are deprived of everything, except the superficial sensation of 'hmm that's noteworthy'. The ability to recognise that something is meaningful, but to not understand *why* it is meaningful, can only be described as a frustrating state of anguish. It's like trying to eat without knowing how to - starvation in the midst of plenty (to use a biochemical analogy). In my experience, this can be attributed to an ignorance of the fact that beauty and meaning come from God. To seek beauty and meaning in something is to seek God.

In the same journal entry, Kierkegaard writes of another problem, when perceiving beauty:
>"The works of the deity are too great for me; I always get lost in the details."

When one possesses the ability to appreciate beauty, and when they then start to look for it, it is easy to become overwhelmed by merely the briefest glance at our world. A single blade of grass is incredibly beautiful, and worthy of a great deal of reflection and introspection, so how does one even approach complex, breathtaking landscapes? The problem is that it is impossible to admire every microcosm and every facet of beauty in the blip that is our mortal, fleeting lives. If one tries to admire everything in full, then every admiration becomes a source of bittersweet guilt and sadness, because there will always be beauty that goes unnoticed. Such is the vastness and complexity of God's creation.
![alt text](/images/2026-07-16-Beauty/image-2.png)
I am not entirely certain on this, but perhaps the solution to this problem is to chase the 'end', not the 'means'. If the purpose of admiring beauty is to bring comfort and joy to the heart and to achieve God consciousness, then any *single* path that you take to achieve that state is sufficient:
![alt text](/images/2026-07-16-Beauty/image-3.png)
If in one moment, admiring the macroscopic beauty of a flower in passing is enough to evoke a deep love for God in your heart, then there is no need to fanaticise over all the flower's intricacies and details. The reason why beautiful things have meaning is because of God. So if you are able to attain love of God through a single instance of admiration, then that beauty has already served its purpose. On the contrary, "getting lost in the details" is a sickness of focus, where the innate source of beauty (which is God) is misidentified as the qualia themselves (the instances of subjective experience), and so the admirer hopelessly chases an unattainable completeness of admiration, and not God, as they should. To put it in lay terms: if things are beautiful because they are instantiations of divine will, then to appreciate beauty is to pursue God, not merely the superficial instantiations that lead to him.

These are the solutions that have freed me from these Kierkegaardian problems, but I may be entirely wrong, God knows.
